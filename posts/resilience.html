Towards a resilience-industrial complex

Context
High military spending

US, Australia and other countries spend a lot of money on conventional military equipment, including 'exquisite assets', that are rarely used except in wars of relative choice.

Ref: How to Defend Australia
Context
Sub-military threats

Other threats to security and prosperity must be confronted, but conventional military assets are of limited help: economic espionage, political interference, natural disasters, pandemics.

Context
Military spending creates jobs

At least some military spending is politically protected because it creates domestic jobs. It needs to be reoriented rather than halted.

Prompt
USNS Comfort in New York

As part of the COVID-19 response, a US Navy hospital ship docked in New York to treat patients. This reflects the potential value of military spending on dual-use assets rather than pure combat assets.

Prompt
US Marine Corps reorients

The USMC is retooling to focus heavily on Western Pacific island warfare, with fewer unnecessary large assets - e.g. giving up all tanks. This reflects that it is possible to change to focus more effectively on a mission, rather than buying all available expensive equipment.

Ref: CSIS analysis
Idea
Create a "resilience-industrial complex"

Encourage transition from military-industrial complex making rarely-useful equipment into resilience-industrial complex making frequently-useful equipment

Objection
We already spend in these areas

Most nations already spend a lot on health (addressing pandemics, natural disasters) and domestic law enforcement (addressing economic espionage). A step change from reorienting defence spending may not be possible.

Objection
Conventional superiority is existential

Even though conventional military equipment is expensive, it's critical: not having it is an existential threat to the nation.

Objection
Impossible to predict needs

Similar to how we often 'fight the last war', it may be just as hard to predict what resilience equipment is necessary. For example, the ventilators needed to fight COVID may not have been at the top of a resilience equipment list.

Response
Focus on cutting military waste

Defence spending is high even in comparison to existing spending in other areas, and wasted resources possibly could provoke a step change. A good initial focus may be winding down programs that are providing little military benefit anyway because they are mis-applied to current warfighting needs - as with the USMC. For example, reducing spending on tanks and increasing spending on cyber warfare -- which may be more relevant to future conflict anyway, and also builds national capacity for non-military defence against cyber-based economic espionage.
